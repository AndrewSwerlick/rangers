# Character Sheet Design Proposal — The Rangers

## Understanding the Game

The Rangers is a one-shot TTRPG (120-180 minutes) where players are park rangers defending humanity's last havens — former state and national parks — against supernatural threats spawned by an alien invasion. The game uses real park maps as game boards, with actual locations (fire towers, dams, ranger stations) serving mechanical roles.

The emotional core isn't about victory. It's about *what you choose to save when you can't save everything*. The three-act structure mirrors this arc:

1. **Prep Phase** — Collaborative worldbuilding. Rangers answer prompts about their past, assign meaning to map locations, and spend tokens on defenses. This is where players invest emotionally in the world they'll defend.

2. **Defense Phase** — Tower defense mechanics. Waves of escalating threats assault the park. Rangers move along trails, man locations, and make tactical decisions under increasing pressure. Resources drain. Positions fall.

3. **Desperation Phase** — When primary objectives fail, rangers make one last stand for personal stakes. Victory is reframed: not "defeat the enemy" but "hold long enough for the evacuation" or "destroy the artifact before they take it." Sacrifice becomes the currency of heroism.

The character sheet must support all three phases while carrying the emotional weight of a ranger's identity.

---

## Purpose of the Character Sheet

The sheet serves five interlocking functions:

### 1. Identity Anchor
Who is this ranger? The art box and CHARACTER section ground the mechanical abstraction in a specific person with a past, a name, fears and hopes revealed through prep prompts. This isn't a generic stat block — it's a portrait in the field guide tradition. The portrait box is left blank for players to sketch their ranger during prep, creating emotional investment through participation. Even rough drawings create more attachment than polished pre-printed illustrations.

### 2. Status Dashboard
HARM and EXHAUSTION trackers show current condition at a glance. As injuries and fatigue accumulate, the sheet itself becomes a record of sacrifice — circles filled in, options narrowing, the ranger's physical state legible without rulebook reference.

### 3. Action Reference
The three-column action layout (PREP / DEFENSE / DESPERATION) organizes abilities by game phase. Players shouldn't need to flip through rules mid-turn. The sheet tells them what they can do *now*, in *this* moment of the game.

### 4. Rules Quick Reference
The HARM EXPLAIN and ROLLS REF sections embed essential mechanics directly on the sheet. For a one-shot game often played with newcomers, this eliminates constant rulebook lookups and keeps players in the fiction rather than the procedures.

### 5. Emotional Object
In a game about sacrifice, the physical artifact matters. A well-designed sheet becomes something players hold onto after the game ends — a memento of a ranger who held the line.

## Thematic & Aesthetic Direction

The typography guide establishes three visual registers that map perfectly onto the game's emotional structure:

| Register | Typography | Emotional Mode | Game Phase Alignment |
|----------|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Monumental** | Josefin Sans Bold | Institutional reverence | Headers, phase names, "RANGERS" identity |
| **Scholarly** | EB Garamond | Precise observation | Rules text, ability descriptions |
| **Personal** | Caveat / player handwriting | Intimate, handwritten | Pre-printed prompts (Caveat); actual entries by player hand |

This three-layer system creates a visual metaphor: the character sheet is an *official document* (WPA monumentalism) that contains *field observations* (naturalist guide precision) annotated by a *specific individual* (handwritten personal voice).

### The Document as Artifact

Think of it as: a Department of the Interior ranger field card, pre-printed with standard abilities and tracking systems, but personalized by the ranger who carries it. The printed elements are institutional; the filled-in elements are intimate.

This duality — official and personal — mirrors the game's tension between the rangers' institutional duty (defend the park) and their personal stakes (what matters to *this* ranger, specifically).

## Key Visual Elements

### 1. The WPA Frame

The outer border should evoke 1930s park service documents: clean geometry, perhaps a subtle double-line border or corner accents suggesting Art Deco influence. Not ornate — *monumental*. The "RANGERS" wordmark at top in Josefin Sans Bold, letterspaced, carrying institutional weight.

Consider: the silhouette of a mountain range, treeline, or park boundary as a subtle header element — the visual language of WPA posters applied to document design.

### 2. The Field Guide Portrait

The art box should be reframed as a naturalist illustration field: a bordered specimen rectangle in the tradition of Audubon plates or Peterson guides — but left blank for the player to fill.

Having players sketch their ranger during prep creates emotional investment through participation. Even rough drawings (stick figures, symbolic gestures) create more attachment than polished pre-printed archetype illustrations. The act of drawing IS prep — it's part of building this specific ranger in this specific moment.

The portrait is the *observation* (what the ranger looks like), while the CHARACTER section is the *record* (who they are, what they carry). Consider a subtle caption line below the portrait for **Ranger Name**, connecting identity to image.

This positions the ranger as both *specimen* (observed, documented) and *observer* (the one who writes field notes).

### 3. Status Trackers

**HARM** — The injury tracker with three circles. Below it, the **HARM EXPLAIN** section provides quick reference for injury thresholds and their mechanical effects (disadvantage, movement restrictions, capture). This keeps the cascading injury rules visible without rulebook reference.

Visual treatment:
- HARM label in Josefin Sans (institutional urgency)
- Three bold circles for marking injuries
- HARM EXPLAIN in EB Garamond at small size — the scholarly register explaining consequences

**EXHAUSTION** — Three circles that parallel HARM's tracking method. Players fill circles as they accumulate exhaustion; the remaining *empty* circles indicate their available dice pool (baseline 3d6). This fill-as-you-go approach matches HARM and avoids erasing — both trackers work the same way, showing deterioration through marks accumulated.

Visual treatment:
- EXHAUSTION label in Josefin Sans
- Three circles, perhaps slightly lighter weight than HARM to suggest fatigue rather than injury
- Small reference note: "(Roll unfilled)" to clarify the inverse relationship

### 4. Rolls Reference

The **ROLLS REF** section (labeled "AND MISC RULES" in the sketch) provides quick mechanical reference: the d6 resolution system (1-3 fail, 4-5 mixed, 6 success), safe vs. risky actions, and other core rules.

This serves a crucial function: The Rangers is designed for one-shots, often with players new to the system. Embedding rules reference directly on the character sheet means the GM doesn't need to repeat explanations and players maintain agency mid-game.

Visual treatment: EB Garamond in small, dense text. Perhaps in a slightly muted color to distinguish reference material from active character information. Could include a simple die icon or roll outcome diagram.

### 5. The Action Triptych

The three-column action layout already mirrors the three-act structure. Lean into this:

- **PREP** — The planning phase. Visual treatment: perhaps a field notebook feel, ruled lines for writing abilities/notes. The space for potential.
- **DEFENSE** — The tactical phase. Visual treatment: perhaps slightly more structured, suggesting operational orders. The space for execution.
- **DESPERATION** — The sacrifice phase. Visual treatment: perhaps more urgent, less ruled structure, fewer lines. The space where rules break down and heroism begins.

Column headers in Josefin Sans Bold. Ability text in EB Garamond. Players write their abilities directly on the ruled lines.

### 6. Archetype Section

The sketch adds a dedicated **ARCHETYPE** section with ruled lines. This is where the ranger's archetype abilities are recorded — the unique mechanics that differentiate a Navigator from a Hunter from a Medic.

The archetype isn't just a label; it's a mechanical identity with specific prep bonuses, defense actions, and desperation moves. Having a dedicated section (separate from the ACTION columns) allows pre-printing of archetype-specific abilities if desired, or player recording during character creation.

Visual treatment: Header in Josefin Sans. The archetype name itself could be in EB Garamond Small Caps (THE NAVIGATOR, THE HUNTER). Ability descriptions in EB Garamond regular.

### 7. Character Section

The largest addition: a substantial **CHARACTER** box for personal details and backstory. This is where prep prompts get answered — "What were you running from?", "Who did you leave behind?", the emotional investment that makes desperation meaningful.

This section serves the "personal" register — but through actual player handwriting, not a simulated handwritten font. The sheets are printed blank and filled in at the table. Caveat's role here is limited to pre-printed prompts that guide without constraining; the actual content is written by players in their own hand, making each sheet genuinely personal.

Visual treatment: Minimal structure — header in Josefin Sans, perhaps one or two prompts in Caveat italic ("*What were you running from?*"), then open space with subtle ruled lines. The emptiness invites filling.

### 8. White Space and Margins

WPA posters and field guides both use generous margins. Don't fill every inch. Let the document breathe. White space communicates: this is a quality artifact, not a cheap handout.

## Implementation Considerations

The current LaTeX template uses TikZ for layout, which gives precise positioning control. To implement this design:

1. **XeLaTeX required** — Josefin Sans and Caveat aren't in standard LaTeX packages. XeLaTeX with fontspec allows direct use of system fonts (installed from Google Fonts).

2. **EB Garamond** — Available as LaTeX package (`ebgaramond`) for pdfLaTeX, or via fontspec for XeLaTeX. XeLaTeX is simpler for a mixed-font document.

3. **Color palette** — Consider a muted WPA palette: park service brown/green for accents, black for primary text, a warm cream/buff background if printing allows.

4. **Landscape orientation** — The current landscape format is correct for a card-style sheet players can reference on the table.

## Summary

The expanded layout from the sketch transforms the character sheet from a simple tracker into a complete play aid: identity anchor, status dashboard, rules reference, and narrative canvas all in one document.

**Left column** handles the mechanical: portrait, status trackers (HARM, EXHAUSTION), and rules reference. This is the operational side of being a ranger — the institutional framework.

**Right section** handles the tactical and personal: action abilities organized by phase (top), archetype mechanics and character backstory (bottom). This is the individual ranger — their specialized skills and their story.

The three typographic registers map across both zones:
- **Monumental** (Josefin Sans) — all headers, creating the institutional frame
- **Scholarly** (EB Garamond) — rules text, ability descriptions, the field guide register
- **Personal** (Caveat + player handwriting) — pre-printed prompts in Caveat; actual entries in the player's own hand

The result should feel like a *found document* from the world of The Rangers: an official field service record, pre-printed with standard procedures and rules, but filled in by a specific ranger — their own handwriting, their own sketched portrait, their own story.

When a ranger falls holding the line at the old fire tower, the sheet they leave behind should feel like something worth keeping.
